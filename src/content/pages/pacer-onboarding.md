## What you're signing up for

Thanks for thinking about pacing me at the Tahoe 200. Here's the straight story before you say yes.

## The basics

- **Pacing = running next to me** between aids for safety and company. You can't carry my gear or hand me food/water on trail.
- **Earliest pickup: mile 87.6 (Loon Lake).** All six legs are Loon Lake to the finish.
- **Legs are 15–27 miles each**, with 2,000–3,700 ft of climbing per leg.
- **Assume night running.** Headlamp is mandatory.

## What it's actually like

By Loon Lake I'll have been out ~30+ hours and ~88 miles. I'll be tired, maybe seeing things, and not witty. Your job:

1. **Keep me moving** — especially uphill, especially when I want to sit down.
2. **Keep me safe** — nav, footing, weather.
3. **Keep me fed and hydrated** — you can't give me calories on trail, but you can nag me at stops and remind me what's coming at the next aid.
4. **Be company** — talk if I want it; shut up if I don't. You'll read the room.

## What to bring

Mandatory pacer gear matches mandatory runner gear:

- Hooded jacket
- Gloves
- Hat or buff
- Pants or tights (full leg coverage)
- Long-sleeve insulating layer
- Headlamp or waist light + extra batteries
- Cell phone
- Whistle
- Emergency blanket
- 500 emergency calories
- Pacer bib (at check-in)
- Your own GPX — don't follow my watch blind

I'd also bring poles, a puffy, 3 L water capacity, and caffeine for you.

## Logistics

- **You get yourself to the aid.** My crew can help coordinate, but travel is on you.
- **Sleep station beds are for runners first.** If a bed opens up, pacers might use it — you might need to give yours up.
- **If you can't keep up, tell me early.** I stay with you to the next aid; you can drop there.

## If you need to bail mid-leg

You can't quit on trail. We push to the next aid together. Pick a leg you'll finish — I'd rather have you on a shorter leg that works than a long one that doesn't.
